A Forbidden-Boundary and Turing-Completeness Argument\\

\begin{abstract}
We present three independent but convergent arguments against the existence of
globally smooth solutions to the incompressible 3D Navier--Stokes equations
extended to rigid-body fluid-structure interaction (FSI). All three arguments
center on the Apollonian sphere packing (APPO) and a family of legally
admissible approximations to it.

The first two arguments form an exhaustive dilemma: APPO is either an
admissible boundary condition for the NS/FSI system or it is not. In either
case, global regularity fails --- in Horn~1 because classical regularity theory
cannot accommodate a fractal domain, and in Horn~2 because the forbidden
APPO boundary forces a fractal separatrix in phase space that is incompatible
with smooth flow.

The third argument is independent and proceeds through computability theory.
We define the family of EPOT (Effective Positive Or Tangent) configurations
--- legal NS/FSI initial data in which finitely many sphere pairs are tangent
and the rest have positive gaps. We show that NS/FSI evolution on the EPOT
family, driven by a flow drawn from the Möbius orbit of a radially inward
field, is Turing complete: it encodes arbitrary computation. By a
diagonalization argument, the boundary in parameter space between initial data
whose evolution terminates (all gaps close) and those whose evolution does not
encodes the halting problem, and is therefore non-computable and fractal.
Globally smooth solutions would make this boundary smooth, which is a
contradiction.

All three routes identify the same fractal object --- the Apollonian residual
set, Hausdorff dimension $d_H \approx 2.4739$ --- as the obstruction to global
regularity.
\end{abstract}

%------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Introduction}
%------------------------------------------------------------------

The Clay Millennium Problem asks whether smooth initial data for the
incompressible Navier--Stokes equations on $\mathbb{R}^3$ always produce
smooth solutions for all time. We approach this question by constructing a
family of smooth initial data for which global regularity cannot hold,
regardless of the analytical properties of the solution operator.

The mechanism is geometric and logical rather than analytic. We exploit two
classical structures:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{The Apollonian sphere packing.} A rigid, algebraically
  determined fractal configuration that sits on the boundary of the legal
  NS/FSI configuration space. Its residual set has Hausdorff dimension
  $d_H \approx 2.4739$ and is nowhere rectifiable.

  \item \textbf{Turing completeness of fluid flow.} Cardona, Miranda, and
  Peralta-Salas~\cite{CMP2021} proved that stationary Euler flows are Turing
  complete: fluid trajectories can simulate any Turing machine. NS/FSI on the
  EPOT family inherits and extends this computational universality.
\end{enumerate}

The main results are stated in Sections~\ref{sec:dilemma}
and~\ref{sec:turing}. Section~\ref{sec:synthesis} unifies them.

%------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Definitions and Setup}
%------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{The Apollonian Sphere Packing (APPO)}

Start with four mutually tangent spheres in $\mathbb{R}^3$ with curvatures
$k_i = 1/r_i$ satisfying the Descartes--Soddy relation
\[
  \bigl(k_1+k_2+k_3+k_4\bigr)^2
  = 2\bigl(k_1^2+k_2^2+k_3^2+k_4^2\bigr).
\]
The Apollonian packing $\mathcal{A}$ is the closure of the set obtained by
iterating the Soddy recursion: at each generation, every interstice bounded
by four mutually tangent spheres is filled with the unique fifth tangent
sphere. The complement $\mathbb{R}^3 \setminus \mathcal{A}$ has Hausdorff
dimension $d_H \approx 2.4739$, is nowhere rectifiable, and is invariant
under the Möbius group $\mathrm{Mob}(3)$.

\subsection{EPPO Configurations}

\begin{definition}[EPPO]
An \emph{Effective Positive Porosity Object} (EPPO) configuration is a finite
collection of disjoint closed rigid spheres whose centers and radii follow
the first $N$ generations of the Apollonian recursion, with every potential
tangency replaced by a strictly positive gap. At generation~$k$ with
$k \geq 1$, the gap between any two spheres that would be tangent in
$\mathcal{A}$ is at least
\[
  \delta_k = \tfrac{1}{2}\min\bigl\{r_j : j \in \mathrm{parents}(k)\bigr\},
\]
with base-generation gaps set to some $\delta_0 > 0$. The fluid domain
$\Omega(0)$ is $C^\infty$; the standard NS/FSI initial-value problem is
locally well-posed.
\end{definition}

\subsection{EPOT Configurations}

\begin{definition}[EPOT]
An \emph{Effective Positive Or Tangent} (EPOT) configuration is a finite
collection of closed rigid spheres in which each pair is either
\emph{strictly separated} (positive gap) or \emph{tangent} (gap exactly
zero). The set of tangent pairs forms the \emph{contact graph}
$G = (V, E)$ of the configuration.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Legality of EPOT initial data]
An EPOT configuration with finitely many tangent pairs is a legal NS/FSI
initial datum.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Justification]
Finitely many tangency points are isolated degenerate points on the boundary
$\partial\Omega$. Standard capacity arguments show that isolated cusp-type
boundary singularities of finite cardinality do not obstruct local
well-posedness: the domain is Lipschitz away from a finite exceptional set of
capacity zero. This is categorically different from APPO, where the
degeneracies are dense and form a set of positive Hausdorff dimension.
\end{proof}

The EPPO family is a subfamily of the EPOT family (all gaps strictly
positive). The EPOT family strictly contains EPPO and includes configurations
with any finite contact graph.

\subsection{Driving Flows}

Fix a smooth, divergence-free, radially inward velocity field $u_0$ on a
control sphere $\partial B_R$ enclosing all spheres. Its magnitude is chosen
so that the induced pressure would close all gaps in finite time in the
absence of rigid-body resistance. The \emph{Möbius orbit}
$\mathcal{F} = \{u_0 \circ \varphi : \varphi \in \mathrm{Mob}(3)\}$
is the full family of driving flows considered. Since $\mathcal{A}$ is
Möbius-invariant, the qualitative structure of the argument is preserved
under $\mathcal{F}$.

\section{The Apollonian Dilemma}
\label{sec:dilemma}
%------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{APPO as a Forbidden Singular Boundary}

As EPPO gaps $\delta_k(t) \to 0$, the intervening fluid layers become
lubrication layers of thickness $\delta_k$. Classical lubrication theory gives
a resisting force scaling as $\delta_k^{-\alpha}$, $\alpha \geq 1$. For
sufficiently strong driving, the driving stress overwhelms this resistance and
gaps close. At the APPO limit:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Infinitely many sphere pairs are simultaneously tangent.
  \item No-slip conditions on adjacent surfaces become irreconcilable at
  contact points.
  \item The domain $\Omega$ degenerates: $\partial\Omega$ becomes the fractal
  residual set of $\mathcal{A}$, which is nowhere rectifiable and not
  Lipschitz anywhere.
\end{itemize}
APPO is therefore not a state in the NS/FSI phase space. It is a
\emph{singular forbidden boundary} of that space.

\subsection{The Dilemma}

\begin{theorem}[The Apollonian Dilemma]
\label{thm:dilemma}
Whether APPO is admitted or forbidden as a boundary condition, globally smooth
NS/FSI solutions do not exist for all smooth initial data in the EPPO family.

\begin{enumerate}
  \item[\textbf{(Horn 1)}] \textbf{APPO is admitted.} Then the NS/FSI system
  admits a solution on a domain whose boundary has Hausdorff dimension
  $\approx 2.4739$ and is nowhere rectifiable. Classical parabolic regularity
  requires $\partial\Omega$ to be at least Lipschitz. Contradiction.

  \item[\textbf{(Horn 2)}] \textbf{APPO is forbidden} (the physically correct
  case). Then the NS/FSI operator on EPPO initial data under the driving flow
  must encode the instruction $\neg$APPO: it must steer all trajectories away
  from the forbidden boundary $\mathcal{A}$.

  The separatrix $\mathcal{S}$ in phase space between initial data that
  approach $\mathcal{A}$ and those that do not must respect the full Möbius
  symmetry of $\mathcal{A}$, and therefore has Hausdorff dimension at least
  $d_H \approx 2.4739$. Smooth flows in phase space produce smooth
  separatrices. A smooth separatrix cannot have fractal dimension. Contradiction.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
Horn~1 is immediate from standard regularity theory.

For Horn~2: the driving flow is radially inward and Möbius-covariant, so it
does not break the Apollonian symmetry of the EPPO configuration. Any
separatrix $\mathcal{S}$ in configuration space that is invariant under
the Möbius symmetry group of $\mathcal{A}$ inherits the orbit structure of
$\mathcal{A}$, which is fractal. If $\Phi_t$ were smooth for all smooth
initial data, the induced flow on configuration space would be smooth,
and smooth flows carry smooth (in particular, finite-dimensional and
rectifiable) separatrices. A fractal separatrix of dimension $\approx 2.4739$
is not rectifiable. Contradiction.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
The two horns are exhaustive. APPO is a rigid, algebraically determined
object: there is no ``partial'' APPO or continuous deformation between
admitted and forbidden. The dilemma is therefore complete.
\end{remark}

\section{Turing Completeness and the Diagonalization Argument}
\label{sec:turing}
%------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{EPOT Evolution Encodes Computation}

\begin{definition}[Gap-closure decision problem]
Given an EPOT configuration with contact graph $G_0$, driving flow
$u \in \mathcal{F}$, and parameters $(i, j, t^*)$, the
\emph{gap-closure decision problem} asks:
\[
  \text{Does sphere } i \text{ make contact with sphere } j
  \text{ at or before time } t^*\,?
\]
This is a well-posed yes/no question about the NS/FSI evolution $\Phi_t$.
\end{definition}

The contact graph $G_0$ plays the role of an initial tape state. New contact
events are state transitions. The driving flow $u \in \mathcal{F}$ is the
program. The evolving contact graph $G(t)$ is the computational trajectory.

\begin{theorem}[Turing completeness of EPOT evolution]
\label{thm:turing}
NS/FSI evolution on the EPOT family, with driving flows drawn from
$\mathcal{F}$, is Turing complete: for any Turing machine $\mathcal{T}$ and
input $x$, there exists an EPOT configuration and driving flow
$u \in \mathcal{F}$ such that $\mathcal{T}(x)$ halts if and only if a
specified gap-closure event occurs in finite time.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
Cardona, Miranda, and Peralta-Salas~\cite{CMP2021} proved that stationary
Euler flows are Turing complete: the trajectory of a particle in a
three-dimensional steady Euler flow can simulate any Turing machine. The
NS/FSI system on the EPOT family contains Euler dynamics as a special regime
(high Reynolds number, rigid bodies as boundary conditions). The contact
graph $G(t)$ provides a physical readout mechanism: a contact event at a
designated pair $(i,j)$ signals the halting of the computation. The
driving flow $u \in \mathcal{F}$ selects the program.

More precisely: the Möbius orbit $\mathcal{F}$ is parametrized by
$\mathrm{Mob}(3)$, which is locally homeomorphic to $\mathbb{R}^6$. This
continuous (uncountable) parameter space of programs contains, as a dense
subfamily, all Turing machines up to the encoding of Cardona--Miranda--Peralta-Salas.
The EPOT family crossed with $\mathcal{F}$ therefore realizes a
\emph{continuous} (analog) extension of Turing computation, at least as
powerful as the model of Moore~\cite{Moore1990} and Bournez--Campagnolo.
\end{proof}

\subsection{The Diagonalization}

\begin{theorem}[Diagonalization against global smoothness]
\label{thm:diag}
If $\Phi_t$ were smooth for all smooth EPOT initial data, then the
gap-closure decision problem would be smoothly decidable as a function of
initial data. But this is impossible.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By Theorem~\ref{thm:turing}, the gap-closure decision problem encodes the
halting problem. The halting problem is undecidable (Turing, 1936). Therefore
the function
\[
  f(u, G_0, i, j, t^*) = \mathbf{1}\bigl[\text{gap}_{ij}(t^*) = 0\bigr]
\]
is not computable as a function of initial data $(u, G_0)$.

If $\Phi_t$ were globally smooth in initial data, then $f$ would be the
composition of smooth maps and would vary smoothly (hence continuously, hence
computably) with initial data. Since $f$ is not computable, $\Phi_t$ cannot
be globally smooth.

More precisely: the boundary in parameter space
\[
  \mathcal{B} = \partial\bigl\{(u, G_0) : f(u, G_0, i, j, t^*) = 1\bigr\}
\]
is the separatrix between halting and non-halting initial data. By the
undecidability of the halting problem, $\mathcal{B}$ is non-computable and
in particular non-smooth. A globally smooth $\Phi_t$ would imply $\mathcal{B}$
is smooth. Contradiction.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
The boundary $\mathcal{B}$ is not merely non-smooth in the sense of lacking
a $C^\infty$ parametrization. By the connection between undecidable sets and
fractal basin boundaries established in the dynamical systems literature
(Grebogi, Ott, and Yorke~\cite{GOY1983}; Smale~\cite{Smale1998}), $\mathcal{B}$
is fractal. In the NS/FSI context, $\mathcal{B}$ is the same fractal
separatrix identified in Horn~2 of Theorem~\ref{thm:dilemma}, now derived
from an independent direction.
\end{remark}

\section{Synthesis}
\label{sec:synthesis}
%------------------------------------------------------------------

The three arguments converge on the same conclusion from independent directions:

\medskip
\begin{center}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.5}
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\hline
\textbf{Route} & \textbf{Mechanism} & \textbf{Conclusion} \\
\hline
Horn~1 & APPO admitted $\Rightarrow$ fractal domain & Regularity fails directly \\
Horn~2 & APPO forbidden $\Rightarrow$ fractal separatrix & Smooth flow cannot avoid fractal \\
Turing & EPOT encodes halting problem $\Rightarrow$ fractal basin boundary &
  Smooth flow cannot decide computation \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
\medskip

In each case the obstruction is the same object: the Apollonian residual set,
Hausdorff dimension $d_H \approx 2.4739$. The three routes are logically
independent: Horn~1/2 require no computability theory, and the Turing
argument requires no detailed knowledge of the Apollonian geometry. Their
convergence on the same fractal object is the main structural insight of
this paper.

\begin{corollary}
There exist smooth initial data for the incompressible NS/FSI system in
$\mathbb{R}^3$---specifically, EPOT configurations with driving flows from
$\mathcal{F}$---for which the solution does not remain smooth for all time.
\end{corollary}

\begin{remark}[Finite-time vs.\ infinite-time breakdown]
The arguments above establish that global smoothness fails; they do not
immediately determine whether breakdown is finite-time (blow-up) or merely
long-time. Upgrading to finite-time blow-up requires showing that the driving
force overcomes the $O(\delta^{-\alpha})$ lubrication resistance for
$\alpha < 1$, so that the gap ODE $\dot\delta \leq -C\delta^\alpha$ reaches
zero in finite time. This lubrication estimate is the primary remaining
analytical task.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Relation to Tao's program]
Tao~\cite{Tao2016} proposed encoding logic gates into NS solutions as a route
to blow-up, and showed that self-similar blow-up is compatible with NS
scaling. The Turing argument in Section~\ref{sec:turing} can be viewed as a
geometric realization of Tao's program: the EPOT contact graph is the
physical substrate in which logical gates are encoded, and the Apollonian
geometry provides the specific fractal structure that triggers breakdown.
\end{remark}
