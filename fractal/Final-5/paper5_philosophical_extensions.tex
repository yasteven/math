\subsection{The Navier-Stokes Problem and Life as Breakdown}

The Clay Millennium Prize asks whether smooth solutions to Navier-Stokes equations exist for all time, or whether finite-time singularities develop. These equations describe fluid motion through momentum and mass conservation:

\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial \mathbf{u}}{\partial t} + (\mathbf{u} \cdot \nabla) \mathbf{u} = -\frac{1}{\rho} \nabla p + \nu \nabla^2 \mathbf{u} + \mathbf{f},
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\nabla \cdot \mathbf{u} = 0,
\end{equation}

where $\mathbf{u}$ is velocity, $p$ is pressure, $\rho$ is density, $\nu$ is kinematic viscosity, and $\mathbf{f}$ represents external forces.

These equations model phenomena from pipe flow to atmospheric dynamics, with analytic solutions existing only for simple cases. For complex, turbulent flows, numerical methods are required—yet even computational approaches struggle with the wide range of scales involved.

\end{itemize}

At each living cell, each conscious decision, each act of agency, the Navier-Stokes equations cease to be predictive. Information appears that wasn't encoded in initial conditions—the deterministic flow encounters a \textit{true} singularity.

\textbf{Implication:} In purely physical (non-living) systems, smooth solutions likely exist globally. In biological systems, singularities emerge at every point of agency. The traditional physics framework is \textit{incomplete by design}—it cannot capture life because:
